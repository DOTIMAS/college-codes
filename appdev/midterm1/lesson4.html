Early Modern Era (c. 1500 - 1800 CE)

The Early Modern Era, spanning from approximately 1500 to 1800 CE, was a period of profound transformation that laid the foundations for the modern world. This era witnessed the Renaissance, a cultural rebirth that revived classical knowledge and fostered advancements in art, science, and literature. The rise of humanism and the printing press revolutionized the spread of ideas, while explorers like Christopher Columbus and Vasco da Gama expanded European influence across the globe. The Age of Exploration and subsequent colonization led to the establishment of vast empires and the exchange of goods, ideas, and cultures between Europe, Africa, the Americas, and Asia, often referred to as the Columbian Exchange. The Protestant Reformation, initiated by Martin Luther, challenged the authority of the Catholic Church and led to the fragmentation of Christianity, while the Catholic Counter-Reformation sought to reform and reaffirm Church doctrine.

The Early Modern Era was also a time of political upheaval, as absolute monarchies and the rise of nation-states reshaped the political landscape of Europe. Key events such as the English Civil War, the Glorious Revolution, and the Thirty Years' War marked significant struggles between monarchs, parliamentarians, and religious factions. Enlightenment thinkers like John Locke, Voltaire, and Rousseau questioned traditional authority and championed ideals of liberty, democracy, and human rights, which influenced revolutionary movements. The Scientific Revolution, led by figures such as Copernicus, Galileo, and Newton, transformed the understanding of the natural world and laid the groundwork for modern science. The period concluded with the American and French Revolutions, which marked the triumph of Enlightenment ideals and signaled the dawn of modern political and social structures.
